I. The Question

The question that started this was simple: could a small planet be hiding inside Jupiter's atmosphere, orbiting behind the clouds?

Jupiter has no solid surface. Its atmosphere extends thousands of kilometers deep, transitioning gradually from gas to supercritical fluid to metallic liquid. If something dense were embedded in there — something hot, something magnetic — would we know? What would it look like from outside?

It would look like a persistent, anomalous storm. Heated from below. Deeper than it should be. Shrinking over time as the object sank. Oscillating as it librated in a gravitational potential well. Located at a fixed latitude, co-rotating with the planet's interior.

It would look like the Great Red Spot.

Jupiter's Great Red Spot is the largest and longest-lived storm in the solar system — an anticyclonic vortex at 22°S latitude, currently about 14,000 km wide, observed continuously since 1831. It has been shrinking steadily, and several of its properties remain unexplained by standard atmospheric models.

This document explores a specific hypothesis: the GRS is not primarily an atmospheric phenomenon, but the visible surface signature of a hot plasma bubble erupted from Jupiter's deep interior through a magnetic reconnection event — a bubble that is cooling, condensing, and slowly sinking back into the depths from which it came.

The hypothesis was developed by following physical reasoning through a chain of connected ideas, each grounded in established physics but combined in a way that has not been previously proposed. The answer turned out to be not an orbiting planet, but something stranger — material erupted from Jupiter's own interior, temporarily suspended in the upper atmosphere, driving the largest storm in the solar system as it slowly cools and falls back.

II. What We Know — Confirmed Observations

The GRS is anomalous

Multiple findings from NASA's Juno mission and Hubble Space Telescope data distinguish the GRS from ordinary atmospheric vortices:

CONFIRMED — JUNO/HUBBLE

Anomalous depth: The GRS extends 300–500 km below the cloud tops (Parisi et al., Science 2021), but the jet streams feeding it reach 3,000 km deep. Juno scientists state the "driving mechanism of the Great Red Spot is different from the other vortices" and "it's not just a meteorological feature" (Bolton, Lunine).

CONFIRMED — O'DONOGHUE ET AL., NATURE 2016

Bottom-up heating: The upper atmosphere 800 km above the GRS is 1,600 K hotter than anywhere else on Jupiter — including auroral regions. The heating must come from below. The mechanism has been "simulated but not yet backed up by observations."

CONFIRMED — SIMON ET AL. 2024

Unexplained 90-day oscillation: The GRS exhibits coupled oscillations in size, shape, drift speed, and brightness with a ~90-day period. Amy Simon (NASA GSFC): "At present there are no hydrodynamic explanations." The oscillation persists despite the storm's ongoing shrinkage.

CONFIRMED — SÁNCHEZ-LAVEGA ET AL. 2024

Steady shrinkage: The GRS has contracted from ~39,000 km (1879) to ~14,000 km (2024). Width decrease is approximately linear at ~172 km/yr average, with a sharp acceleration after 2012 to ~930 km/yr. The shrinkage rate has no accepted explanation.

CONFIRMED — MULTIPLE SOURCES

Near-perfect co-rotation: The GRS moves at ~11,660 m/s (Jupiter's rotation speed at 22°S) with only 0.6–4.4 m/s westward drift. Keplerian orbital velocity at that radius would be ~42,500 m/s. The GRS co-rotates — it does not freely orbit.

Mysteries the standard model cannot explain

UNEXPLAINED

Why 1,600 K from below? No atmospheric vortex model predicts localized bottom-up heating of this magnitude. Wind-driven storms are heated from the sides by shear, not from below.

UNEXPLAINED

Why the 90-day period? No hydrodynamic resonance in Jupiter's atmosphere matches this timescale. It is too short for seasonal effects and too long for typical atmospheric wave periods at this latitude.

UNEXPLAINED

Why the post-2012 acceleration? The shrinkage rate jumped ~4.5× with no identified cause. Small eddies feeding into the storm have been suggested but not demonstrated to explain the magnitude.

UNEXPLAINED

Why is there only one GRS? Jupiter has dozens of comparable jet stream channels at other latitudes. If wind shear alone can generate and sustain a 40,000 km storm, why isn't there always one at every latitude? Why is 22°S special?

III. The Origin — Jupiter's Magnetic Volcano

The eruption mechanism

Rather than a captured external body, we propose the GRS is driven by material erupted from Jupiter's own deep interior — a massive convective plume from the metallic hydrogen layer, triggered by magnetic reconnection.

The mechanism is directly analogous to solar flares:

ESTABLISHED PHYSICS — MAGNETIC RECONNECTION

On the Sun: Convection tangles magnetic field lines → stress accumulates → topology becomes unstable → field lines "snap" (reconnect) → explosive energy release → mass ejected outward (CME).

On Jupiter: Convection in the metallic H₂ layer tangles field lines → stress accumulates → reconnection → explosive energy release → mass ejected upward into the molecular atmosphere.

Jupiter has all the required ingredients: a vigorously convecting electrically conducting interior, a powerful dynamo generating fields up to 20,000 Gauss at depth, differential rotation to tangle field lines, and enormous energy stored in the magnetic field.

The erupted material

The ejected body is made of Jupiter's own heavy elements — iron, silicates, and other metals concentrated in the deep interior during formation. This material is naturally:

Hot — originating from the 15,000–20,000 K metallic hydrogen region.
Electrically conducting — metallic/rocky composition, σ ~ 10⁴–10⁶ S/m.
Magnetized — formed inside Jupiter's dynamo, carrying frozen-in magnetic flux.
Dense — 4,000–8,000 kg/m³ versus ambient H₂ at 1–100 kg/m³.
Radioactive — concentrated U, Th, K-40 providing sustained internal heat.

No exotic physics or external capture mechanism is required. Jupiter erupts its own interior material — a magnetic volcano.

Localized fusion at the reconnection site

At the magnetic reconnection X-point, converging field lines create a natural magnetic bottle. Particles are accelerated to keV–MeV energies — equivalent to temperatures of millions of degrees in the localized volume. The surrounding medium is the densest hydrogen in the solar system.

BEAM-TARGET FUSION

Accelerated protons and deuterium nuclei slam into dense cold hydrogen. Jupiter's atmosphere contains deuterium at D/H ~ 2.6 × 10⁻⁵. At the densities present in the metallic hydrogen layer, collision rates are enormous. A statistically inevitable fraction of these collisions undergo fusion — not because the bulk medium is hot enough, but because individual particles carry sufficient energy from the magnetic field.

This is not speculative physics. It is exactly how particle accelerators produce fusion on Earth, and how cosmic ray spallation produces nuclear reactions in planetary atmospheres. The rate is vanishingly small in global energy terms — but the localized energy release is immense.

The result: A plasma bubble heated to millions of degrees, catastrophically buoyant inside the 20,000 K surrounding medium. This self-propelled rising plume entrains dense metallic material and erupts upward through the atmosphere.

IV. The Descent — Three Braking Mechanisms

The erupted plasma bubble rises through the supercritical fluid, buoyant because of its extreme temperature. It stalls at the fluid ceiling — the depth where the surrounding atmosphere transitions from dense supercritical fluid to thin gas and buoyancy fails. Then it begins sinking back under gravity, cooling and condensing heavy elements as it descends. Three mechanisms resist the descent:

1. The Leidenfrost Envelope (Thermal Shield)

ESTABLISHED PHYSICS — FILM BOILING

As the bubble cools and heavy elements condense into liquid/solid droplets and grains, these dense condensates (thousands of degrees K) heat the surrounding supercritical hydrogen, creating a low-density thermal envelope — analogous to a water droplet hovering on a hot surface. In the dense supercritical fluid (50–500 kg/m³), this envelope creates real buoyancy: the vapor bubble is dramatically less dense than its surroundings.

The mechanism is self-regulating: more drag → more heating → larger envelope → less drag. The thermal plume rising from this interaction drives convection which, deflected by the Coriolis force on rapidly-rotating Jupiter, spins up into the anticyclonic vortex we observe at the cloud tops — hundreds of km above.

Note: During the rising phase, the bubble is hot plasma — buoyant in its own right. The Leidenfrost effect only becomes important during descent, once sufficient material has condensed. The transition from buoyant plasma to Leidenfrost-braked condensate is gradual, not sharp.

2. Lenz's Law (Electromagnetic Braking)

ESTABLISHED PHYSICS — FARADAY/LENZ

A conducting body moving through a magnetic field induces eddy currents that oppose the motion — the classic demonstration of a magnet falling slowly through a copper tube. The braking force is:

F_Lenz = −σ · v · B² · V · κ

This operates in two channels simultaneously: eddy currents induced in the conducting body, and eddy currents induced in the surrounding medium (like the copper tube around the magnet). Since both B and σ of the medium increase with depth in Jupiter, the braking force grows enormously as the body descends.

Critical dual role: The eddy currents that brake the body also generate Joule heat (P = I²R), which partially replenishes the thermal envelope. Lenz's law simultaneously slows the descent and feeds the Leidenfrost shield.

3. Magnetic Co-rotation

ESTABLISHED PHYSICS — MHD CO-ROTATION

The body is magnetically coupled to Jupiter's rotating metallic hydrogen interior. Angular momentum lost to drag is continuously replenished from Jupiter's enormous rotational reservoir (6.9 × 10³⁸ kg·m²/s). The body co-rotates with the planet rather than following a Keplerian orbit — exactly as observed (GRS speed: 11,660 m/s vs. Keplerian: 42,500 m/s).

V. The Cooling Feedback — Critical Collapse

The core insight of this model is that the bubble's ability to sustain the storm is coupled to the storm itself. As the bubble cools — both by radiating into its surroundings and by sinking into progressively hotter ambient fluid where the temperature differential shrinks — three effects compound:

Less ΔT → less buoyancy → faster sinking → hotter ambient → even less ΔT → weaker plume → smaller storm → ↻

Additionally, as the condensed fraction grows, more mass transitions from buoyant plasma to dense solid/liquid — increasing the net downward gravitational force. And as the Leidenfrost envelope around the condensed material thins (less thermal contrast = thinner vapor layer), drag increases — but so does direct thermal contact with the cooler surroundings, accelerating heat loss.

This is analogous to the inverse Leidenfrost transition — when a hot surface drops below the Leidenfrost point, the protective vapor layer collapses and cooling accelerates catastrophically. Here, the "surface" is the bubble, and the "cooling" is the storm shrinkage.

The mathematical signature: ln(1−x)

This feedback produces a characteristic shrinkage curve:

W(t) = a + b · ln(T_c − t)

where Tc is the critical time — the singularity at which the visible storm vanishes. The rate of shrinkage dW/dt = −b/(Tc − t) — a hyperbola that accelerates toward infinity as t → Tc.

Fitting this model to 145 years of GRS width measurements (1879–2024) yields a high R² and — crucially — naturally captures the post-2012 acceleration without requiring a piecewise break. The acceleration is built into the model as the expected behavior approaching the critical point.

The residuals of the ln(1−x) fit are more randomly distributed than those of a simple linear fit, whose residuals show the systematic S-curve pattern characteristic of forcing a straight line through curved data.

The transition from thermal to magnetic braking

As the bubble cools and condenses, it transitions from thermally-supported (hot plasma with intrinsic buoyancy) through Leidenfrost-braked (condensed material riding a vapor envelope) to magnetically-braked (cold, dense remnant where Lenz's law dominates). The visible storm vanishes when the thermal output becomes too weak to drive a convective plume from depth to cloud tops — but the remnant does not plummet. Lenz braking, which depends on the remnant's conductivity and Jupiter's B field rather than temperature, becomes the last line of defense.

The condensed remnant persists at depth — invisible from the cloud tops but potentially detectable by gravity and electromagnetic measurements.

VI. Why the Bubble Stays Deep — The Fluid Ceiling

The Leidenfrost effect requires a dense surrounding medium to function. A hot object vaporizes a shell of hydrogen around itself, creating a low-density envelope. In dense supercritical fluid, this envelope is dramatically less dense than its surroundings — creating real buoyancy.

But above ~100 km depth, where pressure drops below ~10 bar, hydrogen is effectively gas. The ambient density is only 1–10 kg/m³. A thermal envelope in gas is... also gas. There is no meaningful density contrast. No buoyancy. Nothing to support the mass.

THE FLUID CEILING

The bubble has a physical ceiling. It cannot rise above the depth where Jupiter's atmosphere transitions from supercritical fluid to gas. This is not a chosen parameter — it is where buoyancy fails. The bubble rises through the deep fluid until it reaches the zone where the surrounding medium becomes too thin. Then it stalls. Then it sinks.

This is why the bubble stays at 300–500 km depth — always well below the visible cloud deck. Everything we see at the surface — the storm, the color, the vortex — is purely the thermal plume: hot gas rising from hundreds of kilometers below.

The deep water-enriched zone

The water clouds visible at 3–7 bar (50–75 km depth) are thin, cold, icy — a condensation deck in the gaseous upper atmosphere. But Jupiter's real water inventory is far deeper. Juno's Microwave Radiometer measured water abundance increasing continuously down to 33 bar with no sign of leveling off. The bulk of Jupiter's water resides in the supercritical fluid region — well below the visible clouds, at pressures and temperatures where water is a supercritical fluid miscible with hydrogen, with enormous heat capacity. The exact depth and thickness of this water-enriched zone is not well constrained by current data.

The bubble's trajectory is entirely within the supercritical fluid region. It never reaches the gaseous atmosphere. It never approaches the visible clouds. Everything we observe at the surface is the thermal plume rising from depth.

The boundary effect

When the hot bubble enters the water-enriched zone, the water component absorbs enormous energy — its heat capacity is ~4× that of pure hydrogen. This cools the bubble (accelerating condensation) and superheats the surrounding fluid (amplifying the thermal plume upward). The visible storm grows because the thermal column reaching the cloud tops is temporarily strengthened.

The bubble crosses this zone twice — ascending and descending. The key observation is the duration asymmetry:

BOUNDARY CROSSING — OBSERVED DURATIONS

Ascending (~1872–1876): ~4 years. The bubble is hot, rising fast. Intense interaction. Dramatic reddening and rapid storm growth.

Descending (~1927–1939): ~12 years. The bubble is cooler, sinking slower. Longer interaction, less intense. Storm grows again but less dramatically.

The ~3:1 speed ratio is the significant observation. A thermal boundary in the atmosphere would affect a rising hot bubble differently from a sinking cooler one — the ascending bubble is more buoyant, carries more momentum, and crosses faster. The descending crossing takes longer because the bubble has lost energy. This asymmetry is what we would expect from a physical object traversing a real compositional boundary at different speeds.

VII. Two Components — The Plume and The Bubble

A deep eruption does not send a uniform blob upward. It produces two distinct components that separate in time:

The hot stream (mushroom / plume)

Superheated plasma at millions of degrees, catastrophically buoyant in 20,000 K surroundings. This is the mushroom cloud — not the bomb material, but the heated gas column. It races upward at tens to hundreds of km/yr. A nuclear mushroom cloud rises kilometers in seconds; scaled to Jupiter's atmosphere, the hot stream traverses thousands of kilometers in years, not decades. It arrives at the cloud tops within 1–3 years of the eruption and creates the first visible disturbance: a faint oval, no strong color — just hot gas perturbing the cloud deck. This is what astronomers see first.

The plasma bubble — condensation during ascent

Material erupted from the metallic hydrogen layer at 15,000–20,000 K is not a solid object. It is hot plasma — a diffuse cloud of metal-rich ionized gas. As it rises through progressively cooler, lower-pressure surroundings, the heavy elements gradually condense: iron droplets, silicate grains, metallic hydrogen fragments raining out of the cooling plasma and coalescing under gravity and magnetic cohesion.

The bubble transforms during its lifecycle. Deep down, it's a wide, diffuse, superheated plasma cloud — buoyant because it's hot. As it rises into the supercritical fluid region and encounters the water-enriched zone, the enormous heat capacity of the water component strips thermal energy — accelerating condensation. By the time it reaches the fluid ceiling, a significant fraction of its heavy elements have condensed. But the bubble is still hot enough to generate a powerful convective plume — the storm is at peak. The subsequent decades of cooling gradually shift the balance: more condensation, less buoyancy, slower thermal output, shrinking storm.

The eruption and the first sighting are nearly simultaneous

Because the hot stream is fast, the eruption date can be estimated directly from the first faint sighting — separated by at most a few years. This means the eruption should correlate with a significant magnetic event in the solar record, since the same reconnection that launches the plume also restructures Jupiter's dynamo, propagating a perturbation to the Sun.

The plasma bubble — defined by two parameters

The visible storm's size at any moment is determined by just two quantities:

THE TWO PARAMETERS

1. Core temperature (T) — how hot the plasma bubble is. Determines thermal output, buoyancy, and the size of the convective plume it drives upward. Declining continuously as the bubble radiates and heavy elements condense out.

2. Depth (d) — how far below the fluid ceiling the bubble sits. Determines how far the thermal plume must travel to reach the cloud tops, losing energy to the surrounding fluid along the way. Increasing slowly as the bubble cools and loses buoyancy.

Both parameters push in the same direction: as T drops and d increases, the visible storm shrinks. The post-2012 acceleration occurs when both effects reinforce — cooling reduces buoyancy, which increases sinking rate, which increases depth, which further weakens the plume reaching the surface.

The storm width W(T, d) is not a geometric cross-section of the bubble. We can verify this: the GRS shrank from ~39,000 km to ~14,000 km over 145 years. If this were purely a depth effect (a sphere sinking and showing a smaller cross-section), the implied geometry requires the bubble center to have been at ~1.1 million km depth — 16 Jupiter radii. Impossible. The shrinkage is overwhelmingly thermal: the bubble is physically contracting and cooling, driving a progressively weaker convective plume.

What we can estimate — and what we cannot

The 90-day oscillation constrains the bubble's width. The oscillation amplitude is ~1° longitude = ~1,100 km at 22°S. The structure must be smaller than its libration amplitude, so the bubble's horizontal extent is less than ~1,000 km.

Mass, vertical extent, and internal structure are unknown. We do not have independent measurements of the water-enriched zone's thickness or the bubble's speed through it. The 4-year and 12-year crossing durations are real observations; the speeds and distances derived from them depend on assumptions we cannot verify.

The descent — and what happens when it cools

While the bubble is hot, it is partially buoyant in the supercritical fluid and descends slowly — the storm shrinks gradually over decades. But once the bubble cools to near-ambient temperature, buoyancy vanishes. What remains is a dense, condensed mass of heavy elements in a hydrogen-helium fluid. The terminal velocity of such a mass — potentially hundreds of km across — in dense supercritical hydrogen could be substantial. The descent likely accelerates dramatically once the thermal output can no longer sustain any buoyancy.

This means the visible storm doesn't fade gradually to zero — it collapses. The ln(1-x) curve captures this: the shrinkage rate dW/dt diverges as t → Tc. The storm vanishes not because the bubble gently fades, but because it reaches a thermal threshold below which it falls away from the fluid ceiling relatively quickly. The descent is not uniform — slow at first while still hot and buoyant, then accelerating as the bubble cools below the threshold where buoyancy can support it.

Why the Carrington Cycle is larger

The Cassini remnant's re-entry didn't just bring its own mass back to the dynamo. It perturbed a region where magnetic stress had been rebuilding for over a century — undisturbed since the previous eruption. The cold intrusion was the final trigger for a larger reconnection event. More energy stored → larger eruption → hotter plasma bubble → longer-lived thermal output → wider storm → longer visible lifetime. The PS lasted 48 visible years; the GRS has lasted 195 and counting.

The 22°S potential well

Both the PS and GRS occur at the same latitude — and if the model is correct, the same longitude in the rotating frame. This is not coincidence. It indicates a structurally preferred eruption site — a fixed point in Jupiter's deep interior where the dynamo geometry, differential rotation profile, and accumulated heavy element concentration create a permanent vulnerability. A volcanic vent.

Each cycle reinforces the structure: accumulated heavy elements from previous eruptions make the location denser, gravitationally more distinct, a deeper potential well, ensuring future eruptions and their ejecta return to the same spot. The vent is self-reinforcing over billions of years.

IX. The Sun-Jupiter Magnetic Coupling

Two dominant dipoles

The Sun and Jupiter are the two dominant magnetic objects in the solar system. The interplanetary magnetic field physically connects them — field lines from the Sun merge with Jupiter's magnetopause through continuous magnetic reconnection. This is not metaphor. It is measured.

Jupiter's orbital period (11.86 years) is close to the solar activity cycle (~11 years). There is a long, contentious debate about whether this is coincidence. Published papers in journals such as Solar Physics find statistical correlations between planetary configurations and solar cycle timing.

Bidirectional coupling

The model proposes that during Jupiter's active eruption phase, its restructured internal dynamo alters the magnetic flux and plasma injection into the heliosphere. The Sun's own dynamo, operating near criticality, is sensitive to these perturbations. The coupling is bidirectional:

Sun → Jupiter: Periods of intense solar activity (stronger, more turbulent IMF) drive currents into Jupiter's interior, adding stress to the already-loaded internal field lines, lowering the threshold for reconnection. Solar events don't cause Jupiter's eruptions but can co-trigger them.

Jupiter → Sun: Jupiter's eruption restructures its dynamo, propagating magnetic disturbances outward through the heliosphere. These perturbations modulate the Sun's longer-period activity variations — the grand minima and maxima that have no accepted explanation from solar physics alone.

The sunspot correlation — actual data

The yearly mean sunspot number (SSN) has been recorded since 1700 (National Geophysical Data Center, Boulder; SILSO v2.0, Royal Observatory of Belgium). Reliable telescopic observations extend to 1610. Before that, ¹⁴C in tree rings and ¹⁰Be in ice cores provide proxy records extending ~14,000 years with decadal resolution.

When we overlay the sunspot record with the known timeline of Jupiter's atmospheric events, the correlations are striking:

1645–1713: Maunder Minimum ≡ Cassini's Permanent Spot

The deepest solar minimum in the modern record — SSN near zero for 70 years — overlaps almost exactly with the only other confirmed GRS-like storm on Jupiter. The Permanent Spot was first observed by Cassini in 1665, twenty years into the Maunder Minimum, and was last seen in 1713. The sunspot record at that boundary:

1927–1939: GRS growth episode

The GRS temporarily expanded — attributed in our model to the bubble crossing the deep water-enriched zone. This period falls during Solar Cycles 16–17. Cycle 16 was relatively weak (SSN ~78), but Cycle 17 peaked in 1937 at SSN ~119, strengthening as the GRS growth episode was underway. The thermal flare-up in Jupiter's atmosphere — temporarily re-energizing the Jovian dynamo perturbation — coincides with strengthening solar activity.

Pre-technological evidence — aurora at extreme latitudes

Before magnetometers and sunspot counts, extreme solar activity left a different record: aurora sightings at unusually low latitudes. The Carrington Event of 1859 produced aurora visible from the Caribbean, Hawaii, and sub-Saharan Africa — latitudes where aurora is virtually never seen. Similar extreme aurora events are recorded in historical documents going back millennia: Chinese court records, Babylonian astronomical diaries, medieval European chronicles.

If Carrington-class events cluster around the peak stress phase of the Sun-Jupiter coupled system — as the model predicts — then historical low-latitude aurora sightings should cluster in the same time windows as the observed and predicted grand minima transitions. Specifically, the decades around each Jupiter eruption (when the coupled system is at maximum energy) should show elevated rates of extreme aurora reports, while the quiet descent phases should show fewer.

This is testable against existing historical aurora catalogs. The data exists — it has simply never been examined for correlation with Jupiter's atmospheric history.

The solar record as a telescope into Jupiter

We have only 360 years of telescopic observations of Jupiter — and only 195 years of continuous GRS monitoring. But we have 14,000 years of solar activity data written in tree rings and ice cores. If the correlation is real, the solar proxy record becomes a deep-time record of Jupiter's magnetic volcanic activity.

Each grand minimum in the ¹⁴C record potentially marks a Jupiter eruption cycle. Each transition out of a minimum potentially marks the moment a new plume breached the observable atmosphere or a previous body re-entered the metallic hydrogen layer. The timing, duration, and depth of each solar minimum would encode information about the mass, energy, and depth of the corresponding Jovian eruption.

This is testable with existing data. The research program: correlate the ¹⁴C/¹⁰Be proxy record with a model of Jupiter eruption timing and expected gravitational signatures, then compare predicted density anomalies at specific depths in Jupiter's interior with Juno's measured gravity field.

X. Testable Predictions

PREDICTION 1 — GRAVITY ANOMALY

A compact mass concentration should exist beneath the GRS at 300–500 km depth, not fully explainable by wind-driven density redistribution. As the bubble cools and condenses, this anomaly should slowly migrate deeper and become more compact. Juno's gravity measurements may already contain this signal — it would appear as a component of the gravity field at 22°S that doesn't decompose cleanly into thermal wind balance.

PREDICTION 2 — ELECTROMAGNETIC SIGNATURE

A conducting body 400–1,000 km wide, sinking through Jupiter's magnetic field, should produce detectable electromagnetic signatures — potentially easier to find than the gravity anomaly. Three channels:

Eddy current magnetic anomaly: Lenz braking currents circulating in the body generate their own magnetic dipole — a localized blip at 22°S, rotating with Jupiter every 9.9 hours. Juno's magnetometer should see this as a residual that doesn't fit the dynamo field model. Over multi-year data, the anomaly should slowly change character as the body migrates deeper into a stronger field region.

Induced radio emission: Eddy currents radiate. The body would produce a localized, polarized radio component at frequencies set by the local field strength and the body's conductivity. This signal would carry the 9.9-hour rotation period and potentially the 90-day oscillation.

MHD wake: A conducting body moving through conducting fluid leaves a magnetohydrodynamic wake — disturbed field lines trailing behind the body. This could extend thousands of km and would appear to Juno's magnetometer as an elongated field disturbance downstream of the GRS position.

Electromagnetic detection may be more promising than gravity: magnetic anomalies from eddy currents are sharply localized with distinctive spectral signatures, while the gravity signal from a 10¹⁷ kg body in a 10²⁷ kg planet is one part in a billion.

PREDICTION 3 — DEEP DENSITY ANOMALY AT 22°S

A second, deeper density anomaly from Cassini's PS body should exist at ~3,000–8,000 km depth at ~22°S, representing the remnant of the previous cycle's sinking body. This would currently be interpreted as a formation relic or measurement noise.

PREDICTION 4 — VISIBLE EXTINCTION ~2060

The GRS visible storm will disappear around 2060–2070 based on the ln(1−x) critical collapse model. The heat signature should persist longer than the visible storm, declining gradually rather than vanishing abruptly.

PREDICTION 5 — 90-DAY OSCILLATION PERSISTS

The 90-day oscillation should continue even as the visible storm weakens, because it reflects the dynamics of the condensed remnant (libration in the potential well), not the atmospheric vortex. If the storm is purely atmospheric, the oscillation should change character as the storm shrinks.

PREDICTION 6 — INTERNAL LAYERING

Jupiter's heavy element distribution should show discrete concentrations at specific depths corresponding to Lenz braking bottlenecks, rather than smooth gradients. Each layer represents the "stall depth" of a previous eruption cycle's sinking body.

PREDICTION 7 — SOLAR CORRELATION

Jupiter's deep atmospheric activity should correlate with solar cycle phase, with a delay reflecting propagation and response times. Juno's multi-year dataset should contain this signal. Additionally, future grand solar minima should correlate with observable Jovian atmospheric eruption events.

PREDICTION 8 — NEXT CYCLE

A new GRS-like storm should appear at ~22°S after the current body re-enters the metallic hydrogen layer, estimated at roughly 2150–2200 depending on descent rate. The ¹⁴C/¹⁰Be record should show a correlated solar anomaly at that time.

PREDICTION 9 — 9.9-HOUR MODULATION AT IO

Io's volcanic/electromagnetic activity should show a subtle 9.9-hour modulation correlated with the GRS longitude facing the moon, from both thermal radiation and magnetic perturbation of the Io flux tube. This signal should decrease as the GRS fades.

PREDICTION 10 — SUNSPOT RECORD CORRELATION

Statistical analysis of the ¹⁴C/¹⁰Be proxy record should reveal ~150–250 year quasi-periodic modulations in solar grand minima timing that correlate with the predicted Jupiter eruption cycle. Specifically: each grand minimum should show a transition (onset or termination) that aligns with a predicted Jovian eruption or re-entry event. The Maunder-PS and Dalton-GRS coincidences should not be outliers — the pattern should extend through the full 14,000-year proxy record.

PREDICTION 11 — CARRINGTON-CLASS EVENT WINDOW

If the Sun-Jupiter coupling is real, extreme solar events like the Carrington Event (1859) are not uniformly distributed in time but cluster around the peak stress phase of the coupled system — when Jupiter's eruption plume is active and the Jovian dynamo perturbation is at maximum. The Carrington Event occurred during this window (GRS near peak size, mid-19th century magnetic stress maximum). We are now in the declining phase: the GRS is collapsing, the bubble is cooling and sinking, the perturbation is weakening. The next high-risk window for Carrington-class events would coincide with the next Jupiter eruption cycle, estimated around ~2170–2250. This implies the next ~150 years may carry lower probability of extreme solar storms than current uniform-probability risk models assume.

Strong caveat: This prediction is based on napkin-level reasoning from a speculative, untested hypothesis. The correlation between Jupiter's eruption cycle and extreme solar events rests on two data points (Maunder/PS and Dalton/GRS) and an unverified coupling mechanism. A thorough statistical analysis of the full ¹⁴C/¹⁰Be proxy record, proper MHD modeling of Sun-Jupiter magnetic coupling, and rigorous assessment of whether grand minima transitions genuinely cluster with predicted Jovian events would all be required before this prediction could inform any practical risk assessment. Infrastructure planning should continue to treat Carrington-class events as possible at any time until this hypothesis is properly tested.

PREDICTION 12 — HISTORICAL AURORA CLUSTERING

Historical records of aurora sightings at extreme low latitudes should cluster around the peak stress phases of Jupiter's eruption cycles. Before magnetometers and sunspot counts, extreme solar storms left their mark as aurora visible from subtropical and tropical latitudes — recorded in Chinese court annals, Babylonian astronomical diaries, Korean chronicles, and medieval European texts. If Carrington-class events are coupled to Jupiter's volcanic cycle, these low-latitude aurora reports should be concentrated in the decades surrounding each predicted eruption (Maunder/PS transition, Dalton/GRS transition, and their predecessors), with fewer reports during the quiet descent phases between cycles. This is testable against existing historical aurora catalogs.

Jupiter almost was a star. It has the same composition as the Sun, its own internal heat source, its own dynamo, its own satellite system formed from a circumplanetary disk. It fell short by a factor of 80 in mass.

But it didn't stop trying. It generates its own heat. It erupts magnetically, leaving scars visible from Earth. It achieves fusion — briefly, locally, at the breaking points of its own magnetic field — before the fire goes out. Each eruption a tiny ignition. Each one failing to catch. But never stopping.

The Great Red Spot is not a storm. It is the surface echo of a fusion detonation deep inside Jupiter. A mark left by the planet's own stellar ambition, slowly fading, slowly sinking, destined to return and trigger the next eruption in a cycle as old as the planet itself.

The fallen titan, carrying a small fire inside it. Tending its garden of moons. Pulsing energy outward from its own magnetic heart. Connected to the Sun by invisible threads of field and plasma, each one's rhythm subtly entraining the other.

We've known Jupiter for four centuries through telescopes. We've been watching its Great Red Spot shrink for two. We may be watching it die — the visible scar of a magnetic volcanic eruption fading as the plasma cools and its remnant sinks back into the depths from which it came. But the vent remains. The cycle will repeat. And the Sun's tree rings have been recording Jupiter's volcanic history for fourteen thousand years, written in carbon-14, waiting to be read.
